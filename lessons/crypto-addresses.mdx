## Understanding Crypto Addresses

We have already established that Solana, or any other [blockchain](/lessons/blockchain), operates on the principle of transparency. This means that as a regular user, you have access to the whole history of events that happened onchain, and this applies to everyone else too.

There are different types of identifiers and addresses that can help you to analyze those onchain activities. However, to use that information efficiently, you will need to navigate to a block explorer and search what interests you there.

### What Is a Block Explorer?

A block explorer is an interface that allows you to see and read the data stored on a blockchain. With a block explorer you can:

* Track transactions and blocks in real-time
* Look up the transactions history from the moment of creation of the blockchain
* See statistics of usage
* Look up smart contracts
* Search for common identifiers like transaction hash, contract address and wallet address

Naturally you will most likely use explorers for searching, so let's dive into the common types of addresses and identifiers in crypto.

---

### The Public Address

On Solana, your public address or [wallet](/lessons/wallet) address is usually a 32-44 character long string. The public address can be compared to your bank card number because you will have to share it to receive funds from another party.

Knowing your public address, people can look up the history of your transactions, so privacy is not always granted. However, no harm can be done only through the knowledge of your public address.

### The Private Key

What you have to keep in secret is your private key. A private key is a string consisting of 64 characters that verify your ownership of the wallet. It is with a private key that you sign transactions on the blockchain. Through the complex process of cryptography, the private key is converted into a shorter form and becomes your public key. This is done in one way, so public addresses cannot be converted back to private keys.

### Contract Address

Contract address (CA) is a public address that identifies a specific token on Solana. Every token, whether it is a major asset like USDC, a memecoin, or an NFT collection has its own distinct contract address. This is also commonly described as "mint address" or "token address".

You can search up the correct token with a CA to avoid any confusions of its legitimacy. And once again, because of the transparency, you can derive a lot of information from the CA, including the total supply of the token, the owners, distribution and presence of functions like freeze or mint authority.

If you want to trade a token, search its CA on Jupiter's Terminal. The VRFD system will point out potential red flags and assign a green checkmark if the token is safe. Note that the verification only indicates safety for trading. It is by no means an investment advice.

Most importantly, search for the correct CA through official social media sources or reputable websites belonging to a token project.

### Transaction Hash

If a wallet or token has an identifier, the transaction must also have one. It is called a transaction hash, often abbreviated as Tx Hash or TXID (Transaction ID). It is a unique string generated by the blockchain to identify a specific transaction.

It acts as the digital fingerprint or unique reference number for every transaction which is only assigned once and is immutable.

### Token Account Addresses

Another type of identifier in the explorer are token account addresses. They are created when you interact with a new, unique token on Solana, and will deduct a small fee of 0.002 SOL. This is more commonly called "token rent" because you can [reclaim](/lessons/jupiter-reclaim) the fee after you have finished interacting with that specific token.

Token account addresses contain the info on the balance and ownership of the token by a specific wallet, and are helpful in limiting bloating of blockchain size by spammers. In reality, you will likely not see or interact with token account addresses.

### How to Use Block Explorers for Searching Information

**Step 1**

Find a block explorer that's convenient for you. The most used ones are Solscan ([solscan.io](https://solscan.io)) and Orb Markets ([orbmarkets.io](https://orbmarkets.io)).

**Step 2**

Derive information that you want to look up. It will most likely be a public address, transaction hash or contract address. Here's how to find them:

**Public address:** navigate to your wallet and copy the wallet address that you use for receiving crypto.

**Transaction hash:** apps will suggest you to copy the hash after doing an onchain transaction.

**Contract address:** look up the info on official social media sources for a project. If a token doesn't have one, look up the name on Jupiter Terminal and verify safety with a presence of VRFD checkmark, trading activity and absence of major red flags like freeze or mint authority.

Once you have the information, paste it into the explorer's search bar and press enter.

**Step 3**

Analyze the information that you have searched up.

With a wallet address, you can find the hashes of all transactions that you have signed. They will be sorted by types of transactions. You can also see the balance changes, transfers and other information.

As for transaction hash, it can be used to identify smart contracts, pinpoint the timing of the transaction and check the exact balance changes. Having a transaction hash, it is possible to conduct a major investigation on where the tokens flow and who controls them. Another example of usage is looking up the addresses that you send or receive tokens from. Then you can analyze their history of transactions.

Finally, knowing the contract address, you can get the full info on any token and analyze its viability for investments. Developers created tools to investigate inflows and outflows into the token and check the distribution for identifying malicious intents. That's how trading terminals like Jupiter Pro, GMGN or analyzers like Bubblemaps operate.

These identifiers can help you to uncover a lot of data about anything that you want to research in crypto. Just remember that you have to keep your private key secret - the rest you will learn with experience.
